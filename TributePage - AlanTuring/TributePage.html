A short timeline of Alan Turing's life

  • 1912 - Born in Maida Vale, London, in the family of a civil servant.
  • 1926 - His parents sent him to Sherborne School in Dorset. There he started to show his strong interest in math and science.
  • 1928 - At the age of 16, Turing encountered Albert Einstein's work.
  • 1930 - Turing's close friend Morcom suddenly dies. There are speculations that Morcom's death caused Turing to become an atheist.
  • 1931 - Turing goes to study Mathematics at King's College, Cambridge.
  • 1935 - At the age of 22, he was elected a fellow of King's on the strength of a dissertation in which he proved the central limit theorem.
  • 1936 - Turing published his paper On Computable Numbers and an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem (decision problem).
  • 1938 - He received his PhD from Princeton in the US; his dissertation was called Systems of Logic Based on Ordinals and introduced original logic and relative computing.
  • 1939 - During the Second World War, he works in Bletchley Park to develop the Bombe, a device for decrypting the messages sent by the Germans. Turing used statistical techniques to optimise the trial of different possibilities in the code-breaking process using probability.
  • 1941 - Proposes to his co-worker Joan Clarke. Shortly after, Turing had second thoughts, admitting to his fiancee that he was homosexual.
  • 1942 - Worked with US Navy cryptanalysts on the naval Enigma and bombe construction in Washington; During this trip, he also assisted at Bell Labs with the development of secure speech devices.
  • 1943 - Turing and colleagues manage to break the more complicated German Naval Enigma system.
  • 1949 - Turing became deputy director of the Computing Laboratory at Manchester University, working on software for one of the earliest stored program computers – the Manchester Mark 1.
  • 1950 - The ACE (Automatic Computing Engine), a computer designed by Turing, execute its first program.
  • January 1952 - He published The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis, describing aspects of his research on the development of form and pattern in living organisms. Turing used Manchester’s Ferranti Mark I computer to model his hypothesized chemical mechanism for the generation of anatomical structure in animals and plants.
  • March 1952 - He was convicted of “gross indecency”—that is to say, homosexuality, a crime in Britain at that time—and he was sentenced to chemical castration.
  • 1954 - Turing's housekeeper found him dead at the age of 41. Cyanide poisoning was established as the cause of death. An inquest determined that he had committed suicide.

Government apology

  • 2009 - British Prime Minister Gordon Brown, speaking on behalf of the British government, publicly apologized for Turing’s “utterly unfair” treatment.
  • 2014 - Queen Elizabeth II officially pronounced Turing pardoned.
